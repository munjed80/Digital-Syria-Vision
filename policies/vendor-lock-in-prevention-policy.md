# Vendor Lock-in Prevention Policy

---

## Executive Summary

Digital Syria Vision rests on **digital sovereignty**. To protect that
sovereignty, every government technology procurement must avoid structural
dependence on a single vendor, a single proprietary format, or a single
foreign jurisdiction that could withhold service under a sanctions regime.
This policy defines the minimum anti-lock-in clauses that every government IT
contract must include. It is a policy proposal.

---

## 1. Principles

1. **Open standards first.** Every system must communicate over open,
   internationally recognised standards (HTTP, OpenAPI, OAuth 2.x, OpenID
   Connect, SQL, S3-compatible object storage, etc.).
2. **Open formats for data.** Citizen data, audit logs, and configuration must
   be exportable in non-proprietary formats (JSON, CSV, Parquet, XML).
3. **Exit on day one.** Every contract must define, at signature, how the
   government will leave the supplier — data export format, timeline, cost.
4. **Multiple-source viability.** Where a single supplier is selected, an
   alternative supplier or open-source equivalent must be technically viable.
5. **Source code escrow.** For mission-critical custom software, source code
   must be placed in escrow or delivered to the government on milestone.
6. **No data hostage.** Government data must remain government property and
   accessible to the government even after contract termination, including
   during dispute.

---

## 2. Mandatory contract clauses (anti-lock-in)

Every contract for systems connected to NDID, SyriaGovCloud, the Government
API Gateway, or the Government Payment Gateway must include the following
clauses. These clauses are mandatory and may not be diluted in negotiation.

### Clause A — Data ownership and export

> "All data, including derived data, generated by the citizens of the Syrian
> Arab Republic or by Syrian government entities through use of the Supplier's
> system shall remain the sole property of the Government. The Supplier shall,
> on request and at no additional cost, export this data within 30 days in a
> documented, non-proprietary format."

### Clause B — Open standards

> "The Supplier shall expose all functions of the system through APIs based on
> open standards (OpenAPI 3.x or successor). Proprietary protocols may be used
> internally but shall not be required to interoperate with the system."

### Clause C — Source code escrow / handover

> "For any custom software developed under this contract, the Supplier shall
> deposit the complete source code, build instructions, and a working build
> environment in an escrow account approved by the Government, with releases
> triggered by Supplier insolvency, abandonment of the product, or contract
> termination."

### Clause D — No exclusive cryptographic dependency

> "The system shall not require cryptographic keys, certificates, or hardware
> security modules that can only be obtained from the Supplier or from a
> single named third party."

### Clause E — Exit assistance

> "On termination, the Supplier shall provide reasonable assistance, for a
> minimum of 90 days, to migrate to an alternative supplier or to in-house
> operations, including data export, documentation handover, and technical
> support."

### Clause F — No retroactive licensing changes

> "Software licence terms agreed at contract signature shall not be modified
> retroactively to the Government's disadvantage during the term of the
> contract."

### Clause G — Sanctions resilience

> "The Supplier shall disclose any third-party dependency that, if subjected
> to a sanctions regime affecting the Syrian Arab Republic, would impair the
> operation of the system. The Government may require a mitigation plan as a
> condition of contract."

## 3. Cloud and infrastructure

- Workloads must be designed to be portable across at least two cloud
  environments (one of which may be SyriaGovCloud).
- Where managed services are used, an open-source equivalent must be
  identified at design time.
- Container-based deployments (OCI/Kubernetes) are strongly preferred over
  vendor-specific runtimes.

---

## 4. Open-source preference

When functionally equivalent solutions exist, an open-source solution that
can be hosted on Syrian sovereign infrastructure shall be preferred over a
proprietary solution. Total cost of ownership over five years, including
exit costs, shall be the basis for comparison — not licence cost alone.

---

## 5. Review

- Reviewed every 12 months by the NDTC.
- Each ministry CDO is responsible for compliance within their portfolio.
